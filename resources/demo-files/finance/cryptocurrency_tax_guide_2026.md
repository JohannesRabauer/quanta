## Introduction

The IRS has substantially clarified cryptocurrency taxation rules following the 2024 Digital Asset Modernization Act. This guide provides practical compliance strategies for investors, traders, and businesses.

### What Qualifies as Taxable Events

1. **Trading crypto for crypto** (e.g., BTC → ETH)
2. **Selling crypto for fiat currency**
3. **Using crypto to purchase goods/services**
4. **Receiving crypto as income** (mining, staking, airdrops)
5. **DeFi yield farming and liquidity provision**

### What Is NOT Taxable

- Transferring crypto between your own wallets
- HODLing (simply holding without transactions)
- Receiving crypto as a gift (recipient doesn't pay tax; donor may owe gift tax >$17,000)

## Tax Rate Structure

### Capital Gains (Short-term vs Long-term)

**Short-term** (held <1 year):
- Taxed as ordinary income
- Rates: 10% to 37% depending on tax bracket

**Long-term** (held >1 year):
- Preferential capital gains rates
- Rates: 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income

| Income Level (Single) | Long-term Rate |
|-----------------------|----------------|
| $0 - $44,625         | 0%             |
| $44,626 - $492,300   | 15%            |
| $492,301+            | 20%            |

### The HODL Advantage

Example: Purchase BTC at $30,000, sell at $60,000 after 13 months

**Short-term** (if sold at 11 months):
- Gain: $30,000
- Tax (37% bracket): $11,100
- Net: $18,900

**Long-term** (held >12 months):
- Gain: $30,000
- Tax (20% rate): $6,000
- Net: $24,000

**Tax savings from patience: $5,100**

This principle aligns with Helena Ravencroft's investment philosophy: *"Wealth accumulates not through frenetic activity, but through strategic stillness."* (See Investment Strategy 2026)

## Calculating Cost Basis

### FIFO vs LIFO vs Specific Identification

**FIFO** (First In, First Out):
- Default method if not specified
- Earliest purchases sold first
- Often results in higher gains (and taxes) in bull markets

**LIFO** (Last In, First Out):
- Most recent purchases sold first
- Can minimize taxes in rising markets
- Not allowed for stocks, but permitted for crypto

**Specific Identification** (Recommended):
- Choose exactly which units you're selling
- Maximum tax optimization flexibility
- Requires meticulous record-keeping

### Example Scenario

You purchased:
- Jan 1: 1 BTC @ $40,000
- Mar 1: 1 BTC @ $50,000
- Jun 1: 1 BTC @ $45,000

On Dec 1, you sell 1 BTC @ $60,000

**FIFO**: Gain = $60,000 - $40,000 = $20,000 taxable
**LIFO**: Gain = $60,000 - $45,000 = $15,000 taxable
**Specific ID** (choose Mar 1 unit): Gain = $60,000 - $50,000 = $10,000 taxable

Tax savings (FIFO vs Specific ID at 20% rate): $2,000

## Mining & Staking Income

### Proof-of-Work Mining

Mined cryptocurrency is **ordinary income** on the day received, valued at fair market value.

Example:
- Mine 0.5 BTC on March 15
- BTC price that day: $58,000
- Taxable income: $29,000

Later, when you sell that 0.5 BTC:
- Your cost basis is $29,000
- Any appreciation is capital gain
- Holding period starts March 15

### Proof-of-Stake Rewards

IRS clarified in 2024: Staking rewards are income when you gain "dominion and control."

**Practical interpretation**:
- Rewards taxable when you can freely transfer/sell them
- Not taxable if locked in staking contract

**Tax optimization**: Keep detailed logs of unlock dates, not just reward dates.

### Mining Deductions

If mining as a business, you can deduct:
- Electricity costs
- Hardware depreciation
- Internet expenses
- Dedicated space (home office deduction)

**Hobby vs Business distinction matters**:
- Business: Deduct expenses against mining income
- Hobby: Limited deductions, subject to 2% AGI floor

## DeFi Taxation Complexities

### Liquidity Pool Participation

You deposit ETH + USDC into Uniswap pool:

1. **Deposit**: May be taxable event (disposing of assets)
   - Conservative approach: Recognize gain/loss on contributed assets
   - Aggressive approach: Treat as like-kind exchange (risky post-2018)

2. **LP Tokens**: Represent your pool share
   - Cost basis = value of contributed assets

3. **Trading Fees**: Ordinary income when earned
   - Track daily/weekly

4. **Withdrawal**: Capital gain/loss event
   - Compare LP token basis to assets received

**Complexity multiplier**: Impermanent loss calculations for tax purposes remain murky. Consult tax professional.

### Yield Farming

Rewards from yield farming protocols (Aave, Compound, etc.) are **ordinary income** when claimed.

**Strategy**: Consider tax timing when claiming rewards
- Claim in low-income years
- Harvest losses to offset gains before claiming

## NFT Taxation

### Buying NFTs

- No immediate tax consequence
- Starts holding period and establishes cost basis

### Selling NFTs

- Capital gain/loss vs ordinary income depends on classification
- If sold by creator: Ordinary income (like selling artwork)
- If sold by collector: Capital gain (likely)

### The 28% Collectibles Rate Issue

IRS may classify NFTs as "collectibles" subject to 28% maximum capital gains rate (vs 20% for securities).

**Status**: Unclear as of 2026. Conservative approach: Assume 28% rate for high-value NFTs.

### Unexpected wrinkle: NFT royalties

If your NFT generates ongoing royalties:
- Ordinary income to you
- Recipient establishes new cost basis when purchasing with resale royalty attached

## Loss Harvesting Strategies

### The Wash Sale Rule (Currently NOT Applied to Crypto)

**Wash sale rule** (stocks): Can't claim loss if you repurchase within 30 days

**Crypto (as of 2026)**: Wash sale rule does NOT apply

**Tax arbitrage opportunity**:
1. Sell BTC at loss to harvest tax benefit
2. Immediately rebuy to maintain position
3. Claim loss on taxes while preserving exposure

**Warning**: Congress has proposed extending wash sale rules to crypto. This loophole may close soon.

### Strategic Loss Harvesting Example

Portfolio snapshot December 20:
- Ethereum: $10,000 gain
- Cardano: $8,000 loss
- Solana: $3,000 loss

**Action**: Sell Cardano and Solana (realize $11,000 loss)
**Result**: Net $1,000 loss for the year
**Bonus**: Can carry forward losses to offset future gains
**Re-enter positions**: Immediately rebuy ADA and SOL if desired

## International Considerations

### FBAR Reporting

If you hold crypto on foreign exchanges AND total foreign account value exceeds $10,000 at any point:
- File FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR)
- Deadline: April 15 (automatic extension to October 15)
- Penalty for non-filing: $10,000+ per violation

### FATCA

Foreign crypto accounts may trigger FATCA reporting (Form 8938) with higher thresholds:
- $50,000 year-end or $75,000 any time (single, US resident)
- $100,000 year-end or $150,000 any time (married filing jointly)

### Cross-Border Tax Treaty Considerations

Some countries have tax treaties affecting crypto taxation. Example: US-UK treaty prevents double taxation on capital gains.

**Complexity**: Most treaties written before cryptocurrency existed; interpretation varies.

## Record Keeping Requirements

### Essential Documentation

For EACH transaction, record:
1. Date and time
2. Type of transaction (buy, sell, trade, income)
3. Amount of crypto
4. Fair market value in USD at transaction time
5. Exchange/wallet used
6. Purpose (investment, payment, etc.)
7. Counterparty (if known)

### Recommended Tools

**Software Solutions**:
- CoinTracker
- TokenTax
- Koinly
- CryptoTrader.Tax

**Manual Backup**:
- Export CSV from all exchanges annually
- Screenshot wallet balances December 31
- Maintain separate spreadsheet

**Retention period**: IRS can audit 3 years back (6 if substantial underreporting, unlimited if fraud)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Ignoring Small Transactions

Every $5 coffee paid with Bitcoin is a taxable event. Even if unprofitable, reporting required.

### 2. Forgotten Airdrops

Free tokens are income at receipt. Many people forget airdrops from 2021-2023, creating tax liabilities.

### 3. Exchange Bankruptcy Losses

If exchange goes bankrupt (e.g., FTX 2022):
- May be able to claim casualty/theft loss
- Subject to complex limitations
- Generally need to exhaust recovery efforts first

### 4. Not Reporting Mining/Staking

All mining/staking rewards are income, even $50 amounts. Non-reporting risks penalties.

### 5. Mixing Personal and Business Transactions

If you accept crypto for business:
- Track separately from personal holdings
- Different tax treatment (ordinary income)
- May owe self-employment tax

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If crypto trading/investing is significant income source:

**Safe harbor rule**: Pay 90% of current year tax or 100% of prior year tax (110% if AGI >$150k)

**Quarterly deadlines**:
- Q1: April 15
- Q2: June 15
- Q3: September 15
- Q4: January 15 (following year)

**Penalty for underpayment**: ~3-5% annualized

**Strategy**: Calculate estimated gains quarterly, make payments to avoid penalties.

## 2026 New Developments

### Broker Reporting Requirements

Starting 2026, centralized exchanges must report:
- Gross proceeds from sales (Form 1099-B)
- Cost basis (if available)
- Holding period

**Implication**: IRS will know your transactions even if you don't report. Accuracy critical.

### DeFi Protocol Reporting (Proposed)

Congress considering requiring DeFi protocols to report user transactions. Status: Pending as of March 2026.
